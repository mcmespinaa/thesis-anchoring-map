with Proper Anchoring

A thesis introduction is not a list of sections — it is a chain of anchored claims. Every move (the hook, the conversation you join, the problem you raise, the question you pose, the contribution you promise) must be tethered to something a reader can verify: an empirical puzzle, a literature stream, a named assumption, or a stated boundary. Unanchored intros read as opinion; anchored intros earn the reader's next 90% of attention.

The Five-Stage Funnel · narrowing from world → question

Stage 1 · The Hook

Why should anyone keep reading? (Who cares?)

Plant the topic in Pasteur's quadrant — useful AND theoretically rich. Make the reader curious within the first paragraph.

Two archetypes (Grant & Pollock):  The Quote — a startling line, vignette, or industry headline.   The Trend — a shift on Main Street or in the Ivory Tower whose causes remain a mystery.
▼

Stage 2 · The Conversation

What conversation are you joining? (What do we know?)

Establish the field through intertextual coherence: synthesised, progressive, or non-coherent. Show the reader the existing terrain before claiming new ground.

Avoid: "no one has studied X" · "to our knowledge, ours is the first…" — these are first-mover fictions that rarely survive review.
▼

Stage 3 · The Problematization

What is wrong, missing, or contested? (So what?)

Pivot from establishing to problematizing. Strongest intros target problems, puzzles, paradoxes — not gaps. Aim for the inadequacy middle ground: fresh look without burning prior work.

Two routes (Hollenbeck):  Consensus shifting — challenge widely held assumptions.   Consensus creation — resolve a live disagreement.
▼

Stage 4 · The Research Question

What, exactly, will you ask? (Anchored, scoped, answerable.)

RQs are navigational tools, not fixed coordinates (Agee). Expect them to evolve as understanding deepens. A good RQ is specific enough to be answerable, broad enough to be interesting.

Test: can a peer say what counts as evidence for and against? If not, the RQ is still a topic, not a question.
▼

Stage 5 · The Promise & Map

What will we learn — and how will the rest unfold?

Preview the theoretical contribution and signpost the structure. The intro should function as a stand-alone mini-summary: a reader who reads only this should know the question, the lens, the method, and the contribution.

Calibrate: avoid overpromising — a 14% reviewer-flagged pitfall. Match the promise to what the rest of the thesis actually delivers.

The Five Anchors

Every stage on the left must be tied to at least one of these. If a paragraph in your draft can't cite its anchor, it is floating — cut it or re-tether it.

A
Empirical anchor A named phenomenon, dataset, headline, vignette, or shift in the world. Specifies where the puzzle lives. Anchors the hook.
B
Literature anchor A bounded, named conversation — two or more streams you can identify by author and construct. Anchors the field-establishing move.
C
Assumption anchor An articulated belief held by that literature — which you will support, refine, or challenge. The deepest anchor; missing in most intros.
D
Scope anchor The boundary conditions of your study: setting, unit of analysis, time frame, population. Tells the reader what you are not claiming.
E
Contribution anchor The specific way you will change, challenge, or advance the conversation — stated in the intro, delivered in the discussion.

Heuristic: draft each intro paragraph, then write its anchor letter (A–E) in the margin. Any paragraph without a letter is a candidate for cutting.

Lesson 1

Gap-spotting vs Problematization Sandberg & Alvesson (2010)

Gap-spotting

Reinforces or modestly extends existing theory. Common but rarely produces interesting contributions.

  • Incompleteness — "X has been understudied"
  • Inadequacy — "X needs a fresh perspective"
  • Incommensurability — "Prior work is wrong"

Problematization

Names and challenges the assumptions underpinning a literature. Higher upside; harder to do.

  • Identify in-house assumptions
  • Identify root metaphors
  • Identify paradigm-level commitments
  • Identify ideology & field assumptions

For VT26: aim for at least one assumption you can name and gently destabilise — even within an otherwise gap-style frame.

Lesson 2

The Three Reviewer Pitfalls Grant & Pollock (2011)

60%
Failing to motivate & problematize Assuming the topic is "obviously" important; only naming a gap without arguing why filling it matters.
45%
Lack of focus Long, rambling, too many frameworks, describing the paper's structure instead of defining the problem.
14%
Overpromising Selling contributions in the intro that the rest of the thesis cannot deliver. Mismatch is fatal in defence.

Lesson 3

The RQ Refinement Cycle Agee (2009)

Treat the research question as provisional. The cycle below repeats throughout the thesis — not just at the start.

1Curiosity — a topic that genuinely interests you
▼
2Self-questioning — what do I want to know, and why?
▼
3Provisional RQ — broad, open, generative
▼
4Field/literature contact — what does the data push back on?
▼
5Refined RQ — narrower, anchored, defensible
▲ ↻
6Re-examine — does it still match what you found?

Before submission · the anchoring audit

Walk your draft against these twelve checks

  1. Hook is anchored to A or B. Empirical phenomenon or named conversation — not a generic claim of importance.
  2. The "who cares" answer fits in one sentence. If you can't compress it, the topic isn't focused yet.
  3. You name at least two literature streams (anchor B), and you can describe how they relate.
  4. You articulate at least one assumption (anchor C) you intend to support, refine, or challenge.
  5. The word "gap" appears at most once — and never as the sole motivation. Prefer problem · puzzle · paradox.
  6. The research question can be answered with the data you have access to. Otherwise rescope it.
  7. Scope (anchor D) is explicit: setting, period, unit of analysis, what is excluded.
  8. The contribution promise (anchor E) is specific — and you can point to the discussion section that delivers it.
  9. No first-mover claims. No "this has never been done before."
  10. Length budget honoured (~3 pages for AMJ; check your programme's spec). Cut frameworks-of-frameworks.
  11. The intro reads as a stand-alone mini-summary. Hand it to a peer who hasn't read the rest — do they get it?
  12. Rewritten ≥ 5 times. Award-winners average 10. Treat the first draft as a rough cut, not a draft.
